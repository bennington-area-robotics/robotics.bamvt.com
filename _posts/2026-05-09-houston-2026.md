Team 18650 "Cookie Clickers" of Bennington Area Robotics attended FIRST Championship this year for the first time. FIRST Championship is the biggest youth robotics event in the world, and draws over 300 teams in the FIRST Tech Challenge (FTC) league from all over the world. We were one of two FTC teams representing Vermont, along with 8304 "Wired Cats" of Saxtons River.

### Schedule

- Day 0, Tue 4/28: Arrived in Houston at 9, checked into the hotel by midnight
- Day 1, Wed 4/29: Inspections, Practice
- Day 2, Thu 4/30: Qualifiers
- Day 3, Fri 5/01: Qualifiers
- Day 4, Sat 5/02: Playoffs, NASA
- Day 5, Sun 5/03: Return!

### Venue and Environs

The championship was held at the George R. Brown Convention Center in downtown Houston. We stayed in the medical district near Rice University, and took the Red Line to avoid traffic and save money on ground transportation. This took us through various neighborhoods in central Houston, and gave the kids a daily view of urban life in Texas. It was robotics week, and everywhere you looked there was another team in their team uniforms. FIRST Lego League (FLL) teams were young and charming, FIRST Robotics Competition (FRC) teams were large and jolly, and FIRST Tech Challenge (FTC) teams were intense and tight-knit. Everyone was happy to be there, and excited for the competition and festivities.

There were SO many people around the convention center, the organizers claim 50,000 people and I don't doubt it. Such a crowded environment in a downtown American center was a new experience for many of us! Thankfully, all the travel and logistics went off without a hitch, and no one was ever separated from the group or lost in the crowd. Phew!

### The Pits

Within the convention center, FTC was on level 3, in an immense hall divided between competition fields, team pits, and practice fields. Each team had a 10'x10' pit space of their own. Most teams were returning teams and well prepared with a fully outfitted space. This was our first year and we prepared the basics, including tools, team awards and a beautiful banner, but we didn't prepare a canopy, so the cacophony of the hall was always present. Next year a canopy! We carried all our gear with us on the plane, and this limited the kind of items we could bring. Next year, we'll ship our pit gear down separately, with one of the trucks from New England FIRST.

Some teams booked their accommodation in the adjacent Hilton Americas-Houston hotel, some teams even booked a conference room to set up their own private practice field. At first glance this seemed frivolous and extravagant, but when a team invited us to practice with them, it all made sense--that team was fresh and ready to go, they could practice any time throughout the day, and they always had a quiet place to recuperate and work on the robot. The days are long, and the best teams must maintain their spirits!

### The Fields

In the competition hall, the standard 12'x12' playing fields were raised up about 2 feet on black platforms. New and unusual, these provided a better viewing experience for the audience. There were also huge screens with a top-down camera angle for perfect visibility into the field. The competition hall used dramatic spot lighting. This created unique light conditions on the field, and broke our robot's vision system on day two until we recalibrated on day three. We'll pay closer attention next year, and not lose a full day to poor calibration!

### Food

Food was its own challenge, as our hotel didn't have a breakfast buffet, and the convention center was so large that walking to get something took 30 minutes. We figured this out as the week went on, worked more groceries in the meal plan, and even enjoyed some BBQ together.

### Competition

We played in Edison Division, one of six divisions, each named after an important historical scientist or inventor. Edison was the most competitive division, and we lost all 10 out of 10 matches. This was hard on student morale, but they persevered, supporting each other, working on the robot, practicing with alliance partners, and steadily improving throughout the championship. We had a mid-tier robot, and could only hope to win a handful of matches at best, and only if the robot consistently performed well, at 80% reliability or better. 

Leading up to the Vermont Championship, we had two weeks to improve reliability after the mechanical build was complete. Our robot performed well up until the playoffs, when wear and tear from the competition began to cause malfunctions. We still won the Inspire Award--the highest judged award in FIRST--and secured advancement to Houston. Success!

### Redesigns and Reliability

Leading up to Houston, the students were more ambitious, and redesigned several subsystems to raise the ceiling on theoretical performance. They redesigned the intake system for full-width collection, and they redesigned the launch system for volley fire. They even tried redesigning the turret for 360 continuous rotation using a slip ring! The purpose of these changes was to reduce cycle time--how fast can the robot pick up artifacts from the field and shoot them into the goal? Top teams could do this in about a second. At the Vermont Championship, our robot could do it in about 5 seconds--strong enough to place well at Vermont, but not strong enough to be competitive at Worlds.

Alas, failure is the risk of ambition, and we failed to get the robot ready in time for competition. The new subsystems did not come together until the day before we left for Houston, and there was little time to practice and improve reliability. Our robot suffered many mishaps on day two, and we lost the handful of matches we could have won. This was partly my failure as head coach, for not putting up stronger guardrails and providing better guidance, and partly the failure of students, for not tempering their ambitions against the constraints of time and reliability.

Many teams tried big ambitious builds--one team built a whole new robot after qualifying and barely got it working, another team changed too many subsystems and performed worse than before, much to their coach's chagrin.

### Failure as a Teacher

That said, failure is also a great teacher, and youth robotics provides a great venue for students to repeatedly fail in pursuit of a goal, to reach that goal by learning what doesn't work and discovering what does, and to bring something new into the world, of their own unique imprint.

It would have been nice to win at least a couple matches, to testify to the possibility of success at the highest levels of competition. On the other hand, even getting to Houston was a great success, and I am quite proud of the team for doing so. Attending FIRST Championship was at best a reach goal for this year. Congratulations! May you do as well next year, or even better!

### Post-Competition

By Friday it was clear we wouldn't make the division playoffs, and most students wanted a break, so we headed out to Space Center Houston on Saturday to check out the exhibits, including an incredible Saturn V rocket. Other teams had the same idea, and we chatted with one outside the gift shop, both teams nursing their wounds. 

It's easy to feel disappointed with your own performance at Worlds, going from a top team in your regional championship to a middling-or-worse team at Worlds. For graduating seniors it is both glorious to reach Worlds, and bittersweet to be crushed by the intense competition. For juniors and below, partial success provides a reach goal for next year.

Given the level of competition, a better reach goal might be to qualify, and then field an interesting mid-tier robot that is still able to win a few matches--not the best, but darn good, and refreshingly original, with hints of greatness.

### Off-Season

To that end, we'll be meeting over the summer, getting ready for BIOBUZZ, the competition for next year. We don't have the full details yet, but based on practice exercises, the game appears to involve collecting smaller 3" balls, aka "pollen", navigating around obstacles, and delivering the pollen to some destination. 

I have already heard the students talk about "swerve drives," an exotic style of drive base, far less common than the standard mecanum-wheel drive base, and more challenging from a build and control perspective. And why not? Robotics should be fun and interesting, and competition should help focus the mind, but not preclude enjoyment.
